## What Goodtown is (one line)
On-demand, pay-as-you-go private meeting/huddle pods ("a private meeting vending machine") placed *inside* high-footfall destination venues — mixed-use retail (e.g. Ponce City Market), hotel lobbies, hospital lobbies.

## The thesis (condensed + refined)
- Post-COVID hybrid work is sticky; traditional CRE is structurally challenged.
- Co-working (WeWork, and arguably Switchyards) is one response. Goodtown is a different one.
- Gap Goodtown targets: co-working is **members-only**, **not private by default** (open seating), and lives **up a floor in corporate CRE**. Private rooms/booths are an afterthought.
- People already do heads-down work in cafes, libraries, bookstores. Goodtown serves the moment those venues fail: when you need a **private, face-to-face meeting or a high-stakes call** you can't risk taking at home (kids, pets, roommates).
- Modular + compact = fits inside existing venues. This avoids long, inflexible CRE leases (10-yr) in favor of **rev-share with the venue** or **small-footprint rent**. Modularity = mobility = agility.

### Venue category expansion (May 2026)
Original thesis: **high-organic-footfall destination retail** (Ponce City Market, mixed-use, hospital lobbies).

Emerging additional category: **business-density / hotel-adjacent areas.** Downtown ATL, near hotels, World Congress Center, etc. Different demand source: business travelers, conference attendees, transient remote workers, two-party meetings between guests at different hotels. Different go-to-market: hotel GMs, hospitality groups, hotel-management companies. Different deal shapes: guest-amenity partnerships, corporate hospitality.

**Honest narrowing:** the hotel value prop is specific, not universal. Full-service hotels compete with you (their own business centers/meeting rooms). The real fit is mid-tier or limited-service hotels lacking robust business amenities, OR guests needing more privacy than a lobby workspace, OR neutral ground between guests at different properties. Worth a dedicated research thread later; flagged here for now.

### Strategic uses of a venue beyond unit economics
A Goodtown installation can serve multiple purposes simultaneously:
- **Revenue venue** (the obvious one — books and earns)
- **Showroom** (controlled walkthrough for prospective venue partners; solves visualization-objection chicken-and-egg even without strong demand data)
- **Hotel-adjacency prototype** (test the hotel-partnership thesis specifically)
- **Brand/partner trial** (e.g., Switchyards collaboration)

Risk: stacking too many experiments on one venue muddies the learning. **Be explicit about the primary purpose** of any given installation; secondary benefits are bonuses, not evidence.

### Open strategic question: revenue model (B2C vs B2B subscription)
Goodtown's current default is **B2C pay-per-use**. The Throne Labs comparable (B2B subscription for placed infrastructure, sold to municipalities/events) opens a serious alternative path. Detail lives in `workshop_business_model_research.md`. Top-level summary:

- B2C pay-per-use fits destination retail (Ponce, mixed-use) and the original thesis.
- B2B subscription fits hospitals, schools, government, and possibly hotel-as-amenity-buyer. It also navigates institutional procurement (defusing the override failure mode that killed Oakland/CTR) more cleanly than B2C.
- Running both adds complexity that a 2-person team may not be able to sustain.
- **This decision affects every venue conversation Goodtown has**, including the Tavani meeting (specifically for the hotel-adjacency play at 151 Ted Turner Dr NW, B2B partnerships with nearby hotels may work better than B2C pricing).

## Prior venue failures — diagnosed

### The Works (Atlanta CRE)
- Vibe was **brand/positioning fit**, not visualization: they had traditional retail bays available and didn't want a Goodtown "experiment" in their atrium/shared space.
- Implicit framing: "Lease a retail bay like other tenants." They wanted to commoditize Goodtown into a tenant, not treat it as an atrium-amenity partner.
- **Diagnosis:** positioning mismatch. Not a recurring objection across venues; a venue-specific stance on what fits their shared/flagship space.

### The CTR (Atlanta, pre-opening)
- Hard-hat tour with **highly engaged champion**: explicitly open to rev-share, temporary rent, multiple structures; actively ideated pod placement; had clear pressure to fill space ahead of June FIFA World Cup window.
- Then: **ghosted**. No tangible feedback despite earlier flexibility/eagerness.
- **Diagnosis:** ghost-after-enthusiasm ≠ "no signal." It's the signature of an internal stakeholder (legal, risk, senior asset committee, FIFA partnerships team) **overriding the champion**. World Cup distraction is a secondary possibility.

### THE PATTERN (most important diagnostic insight)
**Oakland + CTR share a failure mode: front-line champion enthusiastic → killed/overridden from above by legal/risk/lease/board.** The Works is a different failure (positioning rejection).

- **This is NOT "we need a prototype to touch."** A prototype doesn't fix institutional override.
- **What does fix it:**
  1. Qualify the **actual decision-making process** at a venue before investing relationship capital. Who has to sign off? Legal? Board? Asset committee?
  2. Have a clean **liability/insurance answer ready BEFORE legal asks** — pre-empt the override.
  3. Sell to (or with) the people who can **kill** the deal, not just the people who like it.

### Research target — Jabberbox
- Existing operator of single-user work pods in **airports** (much higher-liability environment than Goodtown's pursued contexts).
- If they've cracked liability/insurance in airports, the playbook is transferable. **Worth a dedicated research pull** to build the pre-emptive legal/risk answer noted above.

### Switchyards facts (verified May 2026)
- ~**37 clubs** (up from ~15 in early 2024), recently opened Raleigh. Aggressive expansion.
- Capital: clean $5M Series D (Bullpen, Apr 2024); aggregators since suggest **~$23M total raised, ~$52M valuation (Sept 2025)** + a Form D ~$12.5M. Verify latest round directly before citing.
- Revenue ~doubling annually since 2021 (per Tavani). Goal: **200 clubs by 2029**, "every residential neighborhood."
- Model: **members-only**, 24/7, unlimited coffee/tea; cafe areas, quiet library zones, **and already-bookable phone booths + meeting rooms**. Small residential footprints (~3,400 sq ft).
- Positioning: NOT "co-working." Tavani frames it as a **consumer membership brand** owning a new "consumer workplace category."

### CORRECTED reframe (supersedes earlier "fill the gap" framing — that was wrong)
Switchyards already has pods and meeting rooms, so Goodtown is **not** filling a missing product. The real wedge is **two structural limits of their model**:
1. **Members-only + residential** can't monetize **high-footfall destination retail** (Ponce City Market, The Works, hotel/hospital lobbies). Nobody buys a neighborhood membership for a place they pass through.
2. So **on-demand / pay-as-you-go isn't a threat to membership — it's the only model that works for transient destination demand.** This is the framing that defuses the cannibalization objection.
- Pitch = "New SKU for a TAM your format structurally can't reach. Modular pods plant the Switchyards brand in destinations with zero CRE lease, extending the consumer brand you're already selling investors."

## Risks / holes to keep in view
1. **"Just need a prototype" may be a narrative cover** for a deeper, unaddressed objection. Diagnose before building.
2. **IP/competitive exposure:** revealing the full vision + Mute Modular relationship + tech approach to Tavani, who could build it himself. Moat is currently thin (a supplier discount + partial booking tech). Sequence what you reveal.

4. **Warm door vs. right door:** desperation pulls toward the warmest available door (Switchyards) rather than the strategically correct one. Worth a sanity check.
